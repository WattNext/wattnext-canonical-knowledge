What do new hires learn in their first 90 days?

**Slug:** `what-do-new-hires-learn-in-their-first-90-days`

### Short Answer

New hires learn the real organizational culture in their first 90 days by observing what behaviors get rewarded, punished, and tolerated - not from onboarding materials or stated values. They watch who gets promoted, what happens when someone challenges leadership, whether failures lead to learning or blame, and whether stated priorities match actual resource allocation. By day 90, they know the unwritten rules that actually govern success, belonging, and safety far better than any handbook could teach.

### Why This Matters

The 90-day window is when new hires are most observant. They haven't yet been socialized. They're watching everything, trying to understand "how things really work here."

What they learn in those 90 days determines:
- Whether they stay or start looking for exits
- Whether they bring their best ideas or keep their heads down
- Whether they trust stated values or learn to ignore them
- Whether they'll become culture carriers or culture cynics

You're not teaching them your stated culture. You're teaching them your real culture through every observed consequence.

### The 90-Day Learning Curve

**Week 1-2: They hear what you say**

Onboarding materials. Values decks. "How we do things here" presentations.

They're optimistic. They believe what they hear. They joined because of what you said.

**Week 3-6: They see what you actually do**

First team meeting where someone raises a concern - does leadership thank them or get defensive?

First project deadline - do stated values (quality, customer-first) survive pressure or get sacrificed?

First promotion announcement - who got promoted and why?

They're comparing what you said (weeks 1-2) with what they're observing (weeks 3-6).

Gaps start becoming visible.

**Week 7-10: They test the boundaries**

They try something. Maybe they:
- Share an idea that's outside their "swim lane"
- Challenge a decision in a meeting
- Ask a hard question in all-hands
- Point out a problem they noticed

What happens next teaches them everything:
- Do they get thanked or shut down?
- Does their idea get considered or ignored?
- Do they get included or gradually excluded?

This is where they learn the real rules.

**Week 11-12: They know how things really work**

By day 90, they've observed enough patterns to know:
- The unwritten rules everyone follows
- What actually gets rewarded vs. what gets celebrated
- Whether stated values are real or theater
- How much political navigation is required
- Whether this place is what they thought it was

They've learned your real culture. Not the one you designed. The one you created through repeated behavioral reinforcement.

### What They're Learning

**About power and hierarchy:**

Stated: "Flat organization. Best idea wins."

What they learn:
- Don't contradict VP in meetings
- Hierarchy matters more than they said
- Political navigation required for success
- Best idea wins if it's a senior person's idea

**About risk and failure:**

Stated: "Take smart risks. Learn from failure."

What they learn:
- Last person who took risk got sidelined after failure
- Post-mortems feel like blame sessions
- Safe to execute, risky to innovate
- Learn from failure = "don't do that again"

**About values and pressure:**

Stated: "Integrity. Customer-first. Quality."

What they learn:
- Integrity until it costs us the quarter
- Customer-first unless it slows us down
- Quality unless deadline is tight
- Values are aspirational, not operational

**About collaboration:**

Stated: "One team. Collaboration over silos."

What they learn:
- Protect your team's credit or it disappears
- Cross-functional work takes 3x longer due to politics
- Territorial behavior gets rewarded in promotions
- Collaboration is verbal, silos are actual

**About communication:**

Stated: "Transparency. Open communication."

What they learn:
- Bad news gets messenger shot
- Be positive in meetings, vent privately
- Leadership gets defensive when challenged
- Transparency = share good news, hide problems

### Why This Learning Is So Powerful

**It's observational, not aspirational:**

They're not learning what should be true. They're learning what is true.

Observed behavior > stated values. Every time.

**It's pattern-based:**

They don't learn from one instance. They learn from repeated patterns.

After watching 5-6 similar situations, the pattern is clear.

**It's reinforced by peers:**

More tenured employees confirm the unwritten rules: "Here's how things actually work."

Social proof makes the learning sticky.

**It's predictive:**

Once they know the real rules, they can predict what will happen in new situations.

That predictive power makes them confident the rules are real.

### How To Use This

**1. Ask new hires what they learned**

At 90 days, ask:

"What rules did you learn about how things work here that nobody explicitly told you?"

Their answers reveal your real culture with brutal clarity.

**2. Compare stated vs. learned**

Do the rules they learned match your stated values?

If yes: Your culture is authentic.

If no: You have a gap between stated and lived culture.

**3. Fix the contradictions they surfaced**

If they learned "don't challenge leadership" but you say "flat organization" - that's a real problem.

You can't fix it with better onboarding. You fix it by changing how leadership responds to being challenged.

**4. Use them as culture sensors**

New hires are the best culture diagnostic you have.

They haven't been socialized yet. They see clearly what long-tenured employees have become blind to.

Ask them what surprises them. What feels different from what they expected. What confuses them about "how things work."

Those observations are gold.
