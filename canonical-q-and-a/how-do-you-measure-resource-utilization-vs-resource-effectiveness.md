# How do you measure resource utilization vs resource effectiveness?

## Short Answer

Utilization = busy, effectiveness = value delivered

## Why This Matters

Most organizations measure resource utilization (are people busy?) without measuring resource effectiveness (are they delivering value?). These metrics often point in opposite directions. You can have very high utilization - people booked solid in meetings and on projects - while being ineffective because nothing important is actually getting finished. Conversely, you can have lower utilization but high effectiveness because people have focus time for deep work and their output is valuable.

Utilization metrics are seductive because they're easy to measure. You can count billable hours, meeting time, project capacity. They make organizations feel like they're being rigorous. But high utilization creates perverse incentives. Teams try to keep people 100% allocated, not realizing that context switching, context setting, and unplanned work all require buffer capacity. Push utilization too high and effectiveness drops because there's no room for focus time, learning, or unexpected problems.

The fundamental problem is that utilization measures input (how much did we invest in this?) while effectiveness measures output (what value did we create?). You can be highly utilized and completely ineffective. You can be lightly utilized and extremely effective. Confusing the two leads to terrible decisions - adding more people to busy teams, filling focus time with meetings, optimizing for busy-ness rather than results.

**Distinguishing utilization and effectiveness requires measuring both dimensions:**

First, acknowledge that 100% utilization is impossible in knowledge work. Real effectiveness requires buffer capacity for context switching, unplanned work, learning, and deep focus. Organizations running at 90%+ utilization show degraded effectiveness because there's nowhere for interruptions to go. Healthy organizations typically show 60-70% allocated capacity, leaving 30-40% for the work that doesn't show up on project plans.

Second, measure what actually matters for your business. Utilization asks "are people busy?" Effectiveness asks "are we making progress on what matters?" This might be revenue, customer outcomes, quality, or strategic progress. If utilization is going up but effectiveness is flat, something is broken. This mismatch is the diagnostic signal that you're confusing activity with value.

Third, track effectiveness per unit of resource. If you're getting less value from the same resources over time, something is wrong - likely either increasing context switching, decreasing focus time, or declining focus on priorities. If effectiveness is steady while utilization increases, you're running unsustainable levels.

**Common failure patterns include:**

Treating utilization as proxy for effectiveness. Trying to keep people 100% allocated. Not measuring what actually matters (effectiveness). Celebrating busyness instead of results. Managing utilization up while effectiveness declines. Adding more people to resolve effectiveness problems, making utilization even higher.
