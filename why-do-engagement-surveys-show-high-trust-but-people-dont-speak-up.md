Why do engagement surveys show high trust but people don't speak up?

**Slug:** `why-do-engagement-surveys-show-high-trust-but-people-dont-speak-up`

### Short Answer

Engagement surveys measure what people feel safe saying, not what they actually do. When people answer "I feel comfortable speaking up" with "agree," they're often responding based on social desirability bias and fear of attribution, not actual behavior. Survey scores can indicate high trust while real behavior demonstrates low psychological safety because the survey itself is a performance of safety rather than evidence of it.

### Why This Matters

**78% of leaders believe their organization has high psychological safety. Only 47% of employees agree** (DDI, 2024). This isn't a simple perception gap - it's leaders making decisions based on false signals while trust reality deteriorates unseen.

Organizations operating with this illusion waste resources on the wrong interventions, miss early warning signals about attrition and performance problems, and systematically underestimate how much organizational capacity is lost to fear and self-protection.

### The Survey-Reality Gap

**What surveys measure:**

When you ask "Do you feel comfortable speaking up?" on an engagement survey, you're measuring:
- What people feel safe saying they feel (not actual safety)
- Responses shaped by social desirability bias (people want to appear positive)
- Fear of attribution (even "anonymous" surveys feel risky)
- Optimistic self-perception (people believe they would speak up, even if they don't)

**What actual trust looks like:**

Real psychological safety shows up in observable behavior:
- Do people surface problems early when they see them?
- Do people admit mistakes without being caught first?
- Do people challenge decisions they disagree with?
- Do people tell leaders uncomfortable truths?

These behaviors are measurable. Survey responses are not.

### Why The Gap Exists

**1. Survey responses optimize for safety**

Answering "strongly disagree" to "I feel comfortable speaking up" feels risky - even on an "anonymous" survey.

People reason: "What if this isn't actually anonymous? What if my response pattern can be identified? Safer to say 'agree' and just keep my head down."

The survey measures risk calculation, not actual safety.

**2. Self-perception doesn't match behavior**

People genuinely believe they would speak up if needed. They think: "If something really important came up, I'd say something."

But when something important actually comes up, they:
- Convince themselves it's not that important
- Tell themselves someone else will raise it
- Wait for a "better time" that never comes
- Rationalize staying silent

Survey response: "I feel comfortable speaking up"
Actual behavior: Silence

**3. Surveys can't capture context**

Speaking up about what? To whom? In what setting?

Someone might feel safe suggesting a process improvement in a small team meeting but terrified to challenge a strategic decision in front of the CEO.

A binary "agree/disagree" can't capture this nuance.

**4. The survey itself creates performance pressure**

Everyone knows engagement surveys matter to leadership. Low scores trigger "culture initiatives."

People optimize their responses to avoid:
- Being seen as "the negative team"
- Triggering unwanted interventions
- Drawing attention to their department

Result: Inflated scores that tell leadership what they want to hear.

### The Real Cost

When leaders trust survey scores over behavioral evidence:

**Attrition becomes surprising**

"Our engagement scores were great. Why did our top engineer quit with no warning?"

Because your survey said "do you feel safe speaking up" and they said "agree."

Their actual experience: Raised concerns three times. Nothing changed. Learned staying silent was safer. Then left.

**Problems surface late**

Projects fail because nobody spoke up about known risks. Leaders ask "why didn't anyone tell me?"

Because your survey said people felt comfortable speaking up. So you assumed the absence of concerns meant everything was fine.

**Capacity gets lost**

When people don't actually feel safe, they spend energy managing perceptions, avoiding risk, and protecting themselves.

Companies with high trust have 74% engaged employees. Low-trust companies: 33% (Edelman, 2024).

That's organizational capacity cut in half - while your survey says trust is fine.

### What To Do Instead

**1. Map actual behavior, not survey responses**

Don't ask "do you feel safe speaking up?"

Track:
- When was the last time someone surfaced a problem early?
- When was the last time someone admitted a mistake without being caught?
- When was the last time someone challenged a decision and got thanked?
- How long does it take for problems to reach leadership?

If these behaviors are rare, trust is low - regardless of survey scores.

**2. Test your response to truth-telling**

The next time someone brings you uncomfortable information, notice your reaction:
- Do you thank them?
- Do you get defensive?
- Do you question their judgment?
- Do you engage seriously with the concern?

Your response in that moment teaches everyone watching whether speaking up is actually safe.

If people see others punished (even subtly) for honesty, they learn - regardless of what the survey says.

**3. Use diagnostic methods that surface real behavior**

ViVo Pulse uses anonymous voice conversations where people describe actual experiences and specific behaviors without attribution.

Instead of "I feel comfortable speaking up" (agree/disagree), we ask:
- "Describe a time when you saw a problem. What did you do?"
- "When someone on your team makes a mistake, what typically happens?"
- "Think about a recent decision you disagreed with. How did you handle it?"

Behavioral questions surface reality. Opinion questions surface performance.

**4. Stop trusting the absence of complaints**

"Nobody's complaining" doesn't mean people feel safe.

It often means they learned complaining is pointless or risky.

The absence of dissent is not evidence of trust. It's often evidence that trust has already failed.
