# Strategic Alignment

> **Why strategies fail in execution and how to translate vision into operational behavior change**

## Overview

Strategic alignment measures whether organizational strategy translates into consistent decision-making, resource allocation, and daily behavior across all levels. Most execution failures aren't strategy problems - they're translation problems.

**Key insight:** Organizations don't fail because strategy is wrong. They fail because strategy remains aspirational, incentives reward old behavior, and leaders mistake agreement for alignment.

## What We Measure

Strategic Alignment assesses **14 indicators** across:

- **Strategy Documentation & Communication** - Is strategy written, accessible, regularly communicated, and understood?
- **Operational Translation** - Does strategy influence daily decisions, OKRs, manager communication, and resource allocation?
- **Strategic Stability** - Are priorities consistent, reviewed appropriately, and visible in tools?
- **Connection & Buy-In** - Do people feel their work contributes to strategy?
- **Trade-Off Clarity** - Are strategic trade-offs explained and used to evaluate success?
- **Customer Linkage** - Do teams understand customer impact and does customer insight shape priorities?

## Common Patterns

### Pattern 1: Agreement ≠ Alignment

**What it looks like:**
- Leadership team agrees on strategy in meetings
- Everyone uses strategic language in updates
- Nothing changes in daily decision-making, resource allocation, or priorities

**Why it happens:**
- Agreement occurs at conceptual level, alignment requires operational translation
- Leaders don't test for execution understanding, only ask "do you agree?"
- Organizational systems (incentives, processes) still reward old behavior

**System-level fix:**
- Translate strategy into specific decision-making criteria
- Update incentives before announcing strategy
- Test alignment at trade-off level, not agreement level

### Pattern 2: Incentive-Strategy Mismatch

**What it looks like:**
- Strategy says "enterprise-first" but comp plans reward any deal closed
- Strategy says "quality over speed" but OKRs measure velocity
- Strategy shifts but promotion criteria still reflect old priorities

**Why it happens:**
- Leaders communicate strategy immediately but update incentives later
- Assumption that mission-driven people don't need aligned incentives
- Concern about mid-cycle disruption outweighs concern about misalignment

**System-level fix:**
- Update comp plans, OKRs, and promotion criteria *before* strategy announcement
- Make trade-offs explicit in measurement (enterprise deals pay 3x commission)
- Test: "If someone optimizes for this metric, do they execute strategy or undermine it?"

### Pattern 3: Resource Allocation Doesn't Shift

**What it looks like:**
- Strategy changes but hiring plans stay the same
- Budget still distributed "fairly" across old and new priorities
- Time allocation in sprint planning doesn't reflect strategic shift

**Why it happens:**
- Leaders avoid "unfair" resource reallocation
- Commitments to legacy work prevent resource shifts
- Resource allocation happens in separate planning cycles from strategy

**System-level fix:**
- Reallocate 70%+ of resources toward top strategic priority within 30 days
- Explicitly cut or deprioritize legacy work
- Make resource shifts highly visible

### Pattern 4: Perception Gaps

**What it looks like:**
- Executives rate alignment 1.6-1.8/2.0
- Employees rate alignment 0.8-1.0/2.0
- Leaders surprised when diagnostic reveals gap

**Why it happens:**
- Signal loss strips away contradictions before reaching executives
- Leaders experience polished, filtered version of reality
- Organizational distance creates blindness to execution-level misalignment

**System-level fix:**
- Compare executive perception with employee experience systematically
- Use anonymous voice diagnostics to surface unfiltered reality
- Test for alignment at execution level, not leadership level

## Research Foundation

Strategic Alignment synthesis draws from:

- **Burke-Litwin Model** - Strategy as primary lever for organizational change
- **Kaplan & Norton (Balanced Scorecard)** - Strategy translation and cascading
- **McKinsey 7-S Framework** - Alignment between strategy and organizational systems
- **Execution research (Bossidy, Charan)** - Gap between strategy formulation and execution

**Key findings:**
- Only 28% of executives say strategic priorities are well-understood at lower levels (McKinsey, 2024)
- Organizations with strong strategic alignment are 70% more likely to be top-quartile performers (McKinsey)
- Misalignment costs average $31.5M annually for companies with 10,000+ employees (Economist Intelligence Unit)

## What Good Looks Like

Organizations with strong strategic alignment demonstrate:

- **Consistent trade-offs without escalation** - Three teams make the same choice using same reasoning
- **Resource allocation matches stated priorities** - 70%+ of resources flow to top strategic priority
- **Minimal perception gaps** - Executive and employee alignment ratings within 1-2 points
- **Strategy shapes decisions 3 levels down** - Individual contributors connect work to strategy and make good trade-offs independently
